## ðŸŸ¢ Phase 1: The Idea & Validation
*The early stage: figuring out what to sell and who to sell it to.*

<Columns cols={1}>
  <Card title="Core Concept: Product-Market Fit" icon="bullseye" iconType="solid" color="green">
    Most students fail because they build what *they* want, not what *others* want. These stories teach empathy and adaptation.
  </Card>
</Columns>

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Case 1: The Sticker Pivot" icon="1">
    **The Lesson: Market Feedback**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Sarah loves drawing anime. She spends $500 printing her art onto T-shirts. Nobody buys them because $25 is too expensive for her classmates. She is devastated.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Pivot">
        She notices students putting stickers on their laptops. She takes her *exact same designs* and prints them as $3 stickers instead of shirts.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        She sells out in 2 hours. She didn't change her art; she changed the **delivery mechanism** to match her customers' wallet.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 2: The 'Uber' for Hall Passes" icon="2">
    **The Lesson: The MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Liam wants to build an app for digital hall passes. He thinks he needs to hire a coder for $5,000. He starts saving money and waits 6 months, doing nothing.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Better Way">
        His mentor tells him to stop waiting. Liam creates a simple **Google Form** and asks one teacher to use it for a week. It costs $0 and takes 10 minutes.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        The teacher loves it. Liam uses that data to convince the Principal to pay for the real app development. **Validate before you build.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 3: The Sneaker Bot" icon="3">
    **The Lesson: Unfair Advantage (Founder-Market Fit)**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Marcus wants to resell Jordans, but he has no money to buy inventory. However, he is really good at coding Python scripts.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Strategy">
        Instead of buying shoes, he writes a "bot" that alerts his friends the second a shoe drops online. He charges friends $10/month for the alerts.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        Marcus built a "Service Business" instead of a "Retail Business." He used his unique skill (coding) rather than competing on cash.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## ðŸŸ¡ Phase 2: Operations & Money
*The middle stage: making sure the business actually makes a profit.*

<Columns cols={1}>
  <Card title="Core Concept: Unit Economics" icon="calculator" iconType="solid" color="orange">
    Revenue is vanity; Profit is sanity. These stories teach students how to calculate the true cost of their time.
  </Card>
</Columns>

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Case 4: The Snow Shovel Trap" icon="4">
    **The Lesson: Pricing Your Time**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Alex shovels driveways for $20. It takes him 2 hours per driveway. ($10/hr). After paying for gas and salt, he is making $6/hour. He is losing money by working.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Fix">
        Alex realizes he needs to charge based on *value*, not time. He changes his price to a flat monthly fee of $150 per house, regardless of snow.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        He gets paid upfront. On light snow months, he makes high profit. His cash flow is stabilized.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 5: The Note-Taking Empire" icon="5">
    **The Lesson: Scalability (Service vs. Product)**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Emily tutors Biology students for $20/hour. She only has 3 free hours a week, so her "cap" is $60/week. She cannot grow.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Scale">
        She scans her best Biology notes and uploads them to a password-protected PDF. She sells the PDF download for $10.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        She sells 50 copies in one week ($500). She did the work once, but got paid 50 times. **Build assets, not just hourly jobs.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 6: The Two Coffee Stands" icon="6">
    **The Lesson: Differentiation**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Jay and Ray set up coffee stands at the football game. They sell the exact same coffee for $2. They split the customers 50/50.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Change">
        Jay decides to sell "Gourmet Hot Chocolate with Marshmallows" for $4. Ray decides to sell "Iced Energy Drinks" for $3.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        They stop competing. Jay gets the families; Ray gets the tired students. Both make more money. **Don't compete; differentiate.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## ðŸ”´ Phase 3: Growth & Ethics
*The late stage: scaling up, partnerships, and doing the right thing.*

<Columns cols={1}>
  <Card title="Core Concept: Leverage & Reputation" icon="handshake" iconType="solid" color="red">
    How to use other people's resources (OPR) and maintain a sterling reputation in a small community.
  </Card>
</Columns>

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Case 7: The Eco-Bracelet" icon="7">
    **The Lesson: Social Enterprise (Triple Bottom Line)**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Mia makes bracelets. There are 100 other people selling bracelets on Etsy. Why should anyone buy hers?
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Hook">
        Mia promises that for every bracelet sold, she will pick up 1lb of trash from a local PEI beach. She documents the cleanup on TikTok.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        People aren't buying a bracelet; they are buying the *impact*. Her customers become her marketing team. **Purpose drives profit.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 8: The Gaming Tournament" icon="8">
    **The Lesson: Win-Win Partnerships**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Sam wants to host a Super Smash Bros tournament. He has a console but no venue and no prize money.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Deal">
        He goes to a local Comic Book store: "If you let me host it on a Tuesday (your slow day), I will bring 50 teenagers who will buy snacks."
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        The store gives him the space for free. The store makes money on snacks; Sam makes money on entry fees.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 9: The Forgotten Password" icon="9">
    **The Lesson: Customer Service & Reputation**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Lucas fixes iPhones. One day, he accidentally wipes a student's phone without backing up their photos. The customer is furious.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Mistake">
        Lucas argues, "You signed a waiver!" The customer posts on Snapchat that Lucas is a scammer. Lucas loses all his business.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Lesson">
        He should have owned the mistake immediately and refunded the money. **In a small town, reputation is your only asset.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Case 10: The Senior Exit" icon="10">
    **The Lesson: Exit Strategy**
    <Steps>
      <Step title="The Scenario">
        Grade 12 student Ben runs a successful lawn care route. He is graduating and going to university. He thinks he has to just "shut down."
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Exit">
        Instead of quitting, he finds a Grade 10 student, Leo, who wants money. Ben sells his customer list and lawnmower to Leo for $500.
      </Step>
      <Step title="The Result">
        Ben gets cash for college. Leo gets an instant business. **A real business has value even when you aren't running it.**
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
